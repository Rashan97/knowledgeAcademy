Introduction to Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the science of energy transformation and the relationships between heat, work, and properties of matter. It governs engines, power plants, refrigeration, and all energy conversion processes. Understanding thermodynamics is essential for designing efficient mechanical systems.

üìå What You'll Learn

  • Thermodynamic systems and properties
  • First Law: conservation of energy
  • Second Law: entropy and irreversibility
  • Ideal gas behavior and processes
  • Power cycles (Rankine, Otto, Diesel, Brayton)
  • Refrigeration and heat pump cycles
  • Thermodynamic efficiency

Fundamental Concepts

Systems and Properties

Thermodynamic System: Quantity of matter or region of space under study Types: - Closed system: Fixed mass, no mass crosses boundary (piston-cylinder) - Open system (control volume): Mass can flow in/out (turbine, compressor) - Isolated system: No mass or energy crosses boundary Boundary: Real or imaginary surface separating system from surroundings Can be: - Fixed or moving - Permeable or impermeable - Rigid or flexible Properties: Characteristics describing state of system Intensive: Independent of mass (T, P, œÅ) Extensive: Depend on mass (m, V, U, H, S) Specific properties: extensive/mass (u, h, s, v) State: Condition defined by properties State postulate: For simple compressible substance, two independent intensive properties define state Process: Change from one state to another Path-dependent or path-independent Cycle: Series of processes returning to initial state Properties return to initial values Net change in properties = 0 Equilibrium: No tendency for change when isolated Types: - Thermal equilibrium (uniform T) - Mechanical equilibrium (uniform P) - Phase equilibrium (no phase change) - Chemical equilibrium (no reactions) Phase: Distinct form of matter (solid, liquid, gas) Homogeneous in composition

Ideal Gas Law

Ideal Gas Equation of State: PV = nRT (molar form) PV = mRÃÑT (mass form) Pv = RÃÑT (specific form) Where: P = absolute pressure V = volume n = number of moles m = mass T = absolute temperature R = universal gas constant = 8.314 kJ/kmol¬∑K RÃÑ = specific gas constant = R/M M = molecular weight Specific Gas Constants: Air: RÃÑ = 0.287 kJ/kg¬∑K CO‚ÇÇ: RÃÑ = 0.189 kJ/kg¬∑K N‚ÇÇ: RÃÑ = 0.297 kJ/kg¬∑K O‚ÇÇ: RÃÑ = 0.260 kJ/kg¬∑K Specific Heats: c_p = specific heat at constant pressure c_v = specific heat at constant volume For ideal gas: Œîu = c_v ŒîT Œîh = c_p ŒîT Relationship: c_p - c_v = RÃÑ k = c_p/c_v (specific heat ratio) For air: c_p = 1.005 kJ/kg¬∑K c_v = 0.718 kJ/kg¬∑K k = 1.4 Ideal Gas Assumptions: - Molecules are point masses (negligible volume) - No intermolecular forces - Valid at low pressure, high temperature Good approximation for air, combustion gases

First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy Conservation

First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted For closed system: ŒîE = Q - W Where: ŒîE = change in total energy Q = heat added to system (+) or removed (-) W = work done by system (+) or on system (-) Total Energy: E = U + KE + PE Usually: KE and PE negligible for stationary systems So: ŒîU = Q - W Sign Convention: Heat in: Q > 0 (added to system) Heat out: Q < 0 (rejected from system) Work out: W > 0 (done by system, expansion) Work in: W < 0 (done on system, compression) Differential Form: dU = Œ¥Q - Œ¥W Œ¥ notation: path-dependent (not exact differential) Per Unit Mass: Œîu = q - w In kJ/kg Cyclic Process: ŒîU = 0 (returns to initial state) Therefore: Q_net = W_net Net heat equals net work for complete cycle

Work and Heat Transfer

Boundary Work (Moving Boundary): For quasi-static process: W = ‚à´ P dV Area under P-V diagram Specific Processes: Constant Pressure (Isobaric): W = P(V‚ÇÇ - V‚ÇÅ) = mP(v‚ÇÇ - v‚ÇÅ) Constant Volume (Isochoric): W = 0 (no volume change) Isothermal (Constant Temperature): For ideal gas: W = mRÃÑT ln(V‚ÇÇ/V‚ÇÅ) = mRÃÑT ln(P‚ÇÅ/P‚ÇÇ) Adiabatic (No Heat Transfer, Q = 0): For ideal gas: PV^k = constant W = (P‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ - P‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ)/(k - 1) = mRÃÑ(T‚ÇÅ - T‚ÇÇ)/(k - 1) Relationships: T‚ÇÇ/T‚ÇÅ = (P‚ÇÇ/P‚ÇÅ)^((k-1)/k) = (V‚ÇÅ/V‚ÇÇ)^(k-1) Polytropic Process: PV^n = constant (n = polytropic index) W = (P‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ - P‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ)/(n - 1) Special cases: n = 0: constant pressure n = 1: isothermal n = k: adiabatic n = ‚àû: constant volume Other Work Modes: Shaft work: W_shaft = TŒ∏ (torque √ó angle) Electrical work: W_e = VI √ó time Spring work: W = ¬Ωk(x‚ÇÇ¬≤ - x‚ÇÅ¬≤)

Second Law of Thermodynamics

Entropy and Irreversibility

Second Law Statements: Kelvin-Planck: Impossible to build heat engine with 100% efficiency (Cannot convert all heat to work) Clausius: Heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot (Requires work input, as in refrigerator) Entropy: Measure of disorder/unavailable energy Differential: dS = Œ¥Q_rev/T Change: ŒîS = ‚à´ Œ¥Q_rev/T Units: kJ/K (or kJ/kg¬∑K specific) Entropy Increase Principle: For isolated system: ŒîS ‚â• 0 ŒîS > 0: irreversible (real) process ŒîS = 0: reversible (ideal) process ŒîS < 0: impossible Clausius Inequality: For any cycle: ‚àÆ Œ¥Q/T ‚â§ 0 = 0 for reversible cycle < 0 for irreversible cycle Entropy Change for Ideal Gas: Constant temperature: Œîs = RÃÑ ln(V‚ÇÇ/V‚ÇÅ) = -RÃÑ ln(P‚ÇÇ/P‚ÇÅ) General: Œîs = c_v ln(T‚ÇÇ/T‚ÇÅ) + RÃÑ ln(V‚ÇÇ/V‚ÇÅ) Œîs = c_p ln(T‚ÇÇ/T‚ÇÅ) - RÃÑ ln(P‚ÇÇ/P‚ÇÅ) Isentropic (Reversible Adiabatic): Œîs = 0 (constant entropy) Isentropic relations: PV^k = constant TV^(k-1) = constant TP^((1-k)/k) = constant Isentropic Efficiency: Real process compared to ideal isentropic Turbine: Œ∑_t = W_actual/W_isentropic Compressor: Œ∑_c = W_isentropic/W_actual Typically 80-90% for well-designed machines

Carnot Cycle and Efficiency

Carnot Cycle: Most efficient cycle between two temperature reservoirs Four reversible processes: 1. Isothermal expansion at T_H (heat in Q_H) 2. Adiabatic expansion (temperature drops to T_L) 3. Isothermal compression at T_L (heat out Q_L) 4. Adiabatic compression (temperature rises to T_H) Carnot Efficiency: Maximum possible efficiency Œ∑_Carnot = 1 - T_L/T_H = (T_H - T_L)/T_H Where T_H and T_L in absolute temperature (K or R) All real engines: Œ∑ < Œ∑_Carnot Coefficient of Performance (Refrigeration): COP_R = Q_L/W_net = Q_L/(Q_H - Q_L) Carnot refrigerator: COP_R,Carnot = T_L/(T_H - T_L) Heat Pump: COP_HP = Q_H/W_net = Q_H/(Q_H - Q_L) Carnot heat pump: COP_HP,Carnot = T_H/(T_H - T_L) Note: COP_HP = COP_R + 1 Practical Implications: - Higher T_H or lower T_L ‚Üí higher efficiency - Power plants: want high boiler T, low condenser T - Refrigerators: want small temperature difference

Power Cycles

Rankine Cycle (Steam Power Plants)

Rankine Cycle Components: 1. Pump: compress liquid to high pressure 2. Boiler: add heat, produce steam 3. Turbine: steam expands, produces work 4. Condenser: reject heat, condense to liquid Ideal Rankine Cycle Processes: 1‚Üí2: Isentropic compression (pump) W_pump,in = h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÅ ‚âà v‚ÇÅ(P‚ÇÇ - P‚ÇÅ) 2‚Üí3: Constant pressure heat addition (boiler) Q_in = h‚ÇÉ - h‚ÇÇ 3‚Üí4: Isentropic expansion (turbine) W_turbine,out = h‚ÇÉ - h‚ÇÑ 4‚Üí1: Constant pressure heat rejection (condenser) Q_out = h‚ÇÑ - h‚ÇÅ Thermal Efficiency: Œ∑_th = W_net/Q_in = (W_turbine - W_pump)/Q_in Œ∑_th = 1 - Q_out/Q_in = 1 - (h‚ÇÑ - h‚ÇÅ)/(h‚ÇÉ - h‚ÇÇ) Typical: 30-40% for modern coal plants 40-45% for combined cycle Improvements: - Superheat steam (higher T‚ÇÉ) - Increase boiler pressure - Decrease condenser pressure - Reheat cycle (expand in stages) - Regeneration (feedwater heating) Back Work Ratio: r_bw = W_pump/W_turbine Small for Rankine (1-2%), favorable

Otto Cycle (Gasoline Engines)

Otto Cycle (Air-Standard): Idealized internal combustion engine Processes: 1‚Üí2: Isentropic compression 2‚Üí3: Constant volume heat addition (combustion) 3‚Üí4: Isentropic expansion (power stroke) 4‚Üí1: Constant volume heat rejection (exhaust) Compression Ratio: r = V_max/V_min = V‚ÇÅ/V‚ÇÇ Typical: 8 to 12 for gasoline engines Thermal Efficiency: Œ∑_Otto = 1 - 1/r^(k-1) Where k = c_p/c_v ‚âà 1.4 for air Higher compression ratio ‚Üí higher efficiency Limited by knock (pre-ignition) Example: r = 10, k = 1.4: Œ∑ = 1 - 1/10^0.4 = 1 - 1/2.512 = 0.602 = 60.2% Actual: ~30% (losses due to friction, heat transfer, incomplete combustion) Mean Effective Pressure (MEP): Fictitious constant pressure producing same work MEP = W_net/(V_max - V_min) Higher MEP ‚Üí more power from given displacement

Diesel Cycle

Diesel Cycle: Compression-ignition engine Processes: 1‚Üí2: Isentropic compression (higher ratio than Otto) 2‚Üí3: Constant pressure heat addition (fuel injection) 3‚Üí4: Isentropic expansion 4‚Üí1: Constant volume heat rejection Compression Ratio: r = V‚ÇÅ/V‚ÇÇ Typical: 14 to 24 (higher than gasoline) Cutoff Ratio: r_c = V‚ÇÉ/V‚ÇÇ (volume ratio during heat addition) Thermal Efficiency: Œ∑_Diesel = 1 - (1/r^(k-1)) √ó ((r_c^k - 1)/(k(r_c - 1))) Efficiency increases with r, decreases with r_c Comparison with Otto: For same r: Œ∑_Otto > Œ∑_Diesel But diesel uses much higher r, so overall Œ∑_Diesel > Œ∑_Otto Diesel: ~40-45% efficiency Otto: ~25-30% efficiency Diesel advantages: - Higher efficiency - Higher torque - No spark plugs (simpler ignition) Diesel disadvantages: - Heavier, more expensive - Higher emissions (NOx, particulates) - Noisier operation

Brayton Cycle (Gas Turbines)

Brayton Cycle: Jet engines, gas turbine power plants Open cycle (actual) or closed cycle (ideal analysis) Processes: 1‚Üí2: Isentropic compression (compressor) 2‚Üí3: Constant pressure heat addition (combustor) 3‚Üí4: Isentropic expansion (turbine) 4‚Üí1: Constant pressure heat rejection (to atmosphere) Pressure Ratio: r_p = P‚ÇÇ/P‚ÇÅ Typical: 10 to 30 for aircraft engines Thermal Efficiency: Œ∑_Brayton = 1 - 1/r_p^((k-1)/k) For k = 1.4: Œ∑ = 1 - 1/r_p^0.286 Back Work Ratio: Large (40-80% of turbine work drives compressor) Unfavorable compared to Rankine Improvements: - Intercooling (cool between compressor stages) - Reheating (reheat between turbine stages) - Regeneration (use turbine exhaust to preheat air) Combined cycle: Gas turbine + steam turbine (use exhaust heat) Efficiency up to 60% Applications: - Aircraft propulsion (turbojets, turbofans) - Peak power plants (fast startup) - Combined cycle power generation

Refrigeration Cycles

Vapor-Compression Refrigeration

Refrigeration Cycle Components: 1. Compressor: compress vapor to high pressure 2. Condenser: reject heat, condense to liquid 3. Expansion valve: throttle to low pressure 4. Evaporator: absorb heat, evaporate to vapor Ideal Cycle Processes: 1‚Üí2: Isentropic compression W_comp = h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÅ 2‚Üí3: Constant pressure heat rejection (condenser) Q_out = h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÉ 3‚Üí4: Throttling (isenthalpic) h‚ÇÑ = h‚ÇÉ (but pressure and temperature drop) 4‚Üí1: Constant pressure heat absorption (evaporator) Q_in = h‚ÇÅ - h‚ÇÑ Coefficient of Performance: COP_R = Q_in/W_comp = (h‚ÇÅ - h‚ÇÑ)/(h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÅ) Typical: 2 to 6 (higher is better) Means: For 1 kW compressor work, remove 2-6 kW heat Refrigerants: Properties needed: - Low boiling point - High latent heat - Non-toxic, non-flammable - Environmentally safe Common: R-134a (automotive AC, replaced R-12) R-410A (residential AC, replaced R-22) Ammonia (industrial) CO‚ÇÇ (natural refrigerant) Ton of Refrigeration: 1 ton = 211 kJ/min = 3.517 kW Based on melting 1 ton of ice in 24 hours Heat Pump: Same cycle, but goal is heating (Q_out) COP_HP = Q_out/W_comp = (h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÉ)/(h‚ÇÇ - h‚ÇÅ) COP_HP = COP_R + 1 More efficient than electric resistance heating

Summary

  • Properties define state; two independent properties fix state for simple substance
  • First Law: ŒîU = Q - W (energy conservation), Q and W are path-dependent
  • Ideal gas: Pv = RÃÑT, Œîu = c_vŒîT, Œîh = c_pŒîT, great approximation for air
  • Second Law: entropy always increases for isolated system, defines maximum efficiency
  • Carnot: most efficient cycle, Œ∑ = 1 - T_L/T_H (all real engines less efficient)
  • Rankine (steam): boiler-turbine-condenser-pump, Œ∑ ‚âà 30-40%, backbone of power generation
  • Otto (gasoline): constant volume combustion, Œ∑ = 1 - 1/r^(k-1), limited by knock
  • Diesel: constant pressure combustion, higher compression, higher efficiency than Otto
  • Brayton (gas turbine): compressor-combustor-turbine, high power/weight ratio
  • Refrigeration: vapor compression cycle, COP = cooling/work, heat pumps more efficient than resistive heating
