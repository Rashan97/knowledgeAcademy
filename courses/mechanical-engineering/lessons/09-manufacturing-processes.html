Introduction to Manufacturing

Manufacturing transforms raw materials into finished products through various physical and chemical processes. Understanding manufacturing capabilities and limitations is essential for designing parts that are functional, economical, and manufacturable.

üìå What You'll Learn

  • Casting processes (sand, die, investment)
  • Bulk deformation (forging, rolling, extrusion)
  • Sheet metal forming (bending, deep drawing)
  • Machining fundamentals (turning, milling, drilling)
  • Cutting parameters and tool life
  • Non-traditional machining (EDM, laser, waterjet)
  • Joining processes (welding, brazing, soldering)
  • Additive manufacturing (3D printing)

Casting Processes

Fundamentals of Casting

Casting Process: Pour molten metal into mold cavity Allow to solidify Remove part (and possibly mold) Advantages: + Complex geometries + Large parts possible + Internal cavities + Near-net shape (less machining) + Wide material selection Disadvantages: - Porosity possible - Rough surface finish - Less precise tolerances - Solidification defects Mold Materials: Expendable: Sand, plaster, ceramic (one use) Permanent: Metal, graphite (multiple uses) Design Considerations: Shrinkage: Metal contracts during solidification Typical shrinkage allowances: Cast iron: 1% Steel: 2% Aluminum: 1.3% Brass: 1.5% Pattern oversized by shrinkage % Draft: Taper for part removal Typical: 1-3¬∞ for sand, 2-5¬∞ for permanent mold Fillets and Radii: Avoid sharp corners (stress concentration, cracking) Minimum radius ‚âà 3 mm Uniform Wall Thickness: Uneven thickness ‚Üí differential cooling ‚Üí defects If variation needed: gradual transition Parting Line: Where mold halves meet Choose to minimize complexity Gating and Risers: Gating: Channel for molten metal entry Riser (feeder): Reservoir to compensate shrinkage Proper design prevents: - Shrinkage cavities - Incomplete filling - Trapped gas

Casting Methods

Sand Casting: Most common (60% of all castings) Process: 1. Make pattern (wood, plastic, metal) 2. Pack sand around pattern (green sand = clay binder) 3. Remove pattern 4. Create gating system 5. Pour metal 6. Cool, break mold, remove casting Advantages: + Low cost for small quantities + Large parts (engine blocks, machine bases) + All metals Disadvantages: - Rough surface (Ra ‚âà 12-25 Œºm) - Wide tolerances (¬±1-2 mm) - Labor-intensive Variations: Shell molding: Thin shell (better finish) No-bake: Chemical binder (no baking) Die Casting: Permanent metal mold, pressure injection Process: 1. Close steel dies 2. Inject molten metal under pressure (10-150 MPa) 3. Cool rapidly 4. Open dies, eject part Advantages: + Excellent surface finish (Ra ‚âà 0.8-1.6 Œºm) + Tight tolerances (¬±0.1-0.2 mm) + High production rate + Thin walls possible (0.5 mm) Disadvantages: - High tooling cost - Limited to low-melting alloys (Zn, Al, Mg, Cu) - Part size limited (typically < 50 kg) Types: Hot chamber: Metal in machine (Zn, Mg, Pb) Cold chamber: Ladled metal (Al, Cu, high-melting) Investment Casting (Lost Wax): Precision casting Process: 1. Inject wax into metal die (pattern) 2. Assemble wax patterns on tree 3. Coat with ceramic slurry, dry (shell) 4. Melt out wax 5. Preheat shell, pour metal 6. Break shell, remove parts Advantages: + Excellent accuracy (¬±0.05-0.15 mm) + Smooth surface (Ra ‚âà 1.6-3.2 Œºm) + Complex geometries, undercuts + All metals (including high-temp alloys) Disadvantages: - Expensive - Small-medium parts - Longer lead time Applications: Turbine blades, dental crowns, jewelry Permanent Mold Casting: Reusable metal mold, gravity pour Better than sand: Finer grain, better properties Lower cost than die casting (no high pressure) Variations: Centrifugal: Rotating mold (pipes, rings) Low-pressure: Slight pressure for filling Continuous Casting: Solidify while withdrawing from mold Products: Slabs, billets, blooms (semi-finished) Used in steel and aluminum production

Bulk Deformation Processes

Forging

Forging: Plastically deform metal using compressive forces Advantages: + Improved grain flow (strength) + Refined grain structure + Elimination of defects + High strength-to-weight Temperature: Hot forging: Above recrystallization T Easier deformation, lower forces Steel: 1100-1250¬∞C Cold forging: Room temperature Higher strength, better finish Work hardening occurs Open-Die Forging: Simple dies, no complete cavity Operations: Upsetting: Compress height, increase diameter Drawing out: Elongate, reduce cross-section Applications: Large parts (shafts, turbine discs) Custom, low volume Closed-Die (Impression-Die) Forging: Metal forced into die cavity Process: 1. Heat billet 2. Place in bottom die 3. Top die descends, metal flows into cavity 4. Flash forms (excess metal in gap) 5. Trim flash Flash: Beneficial: Restricts flow, fills cavity Removed: Trimming operation Draft: 3-10¬∞ (for removal) Parting line: Die separation plane Applications: Automotive (crankshafts, connecting rods) Aerospace (landing gear) Tools (wrenches, hammers) Forging Equipment: Hammers: Impact force, kinetic energy Drop hammer, power hammer Presses: Squeezing force, hydraulic/mechanical Slower, controlled deformation Design Guidelines: - Avoid sharp corners (use radii) - Uniform thickness preferred - Consider grain flow direction - Parting line on largest dimension - Draft angles for removal

Sheet Metal Forming

Bending and Forming Operations

Machining Processes

Fundamentals of Machining

Machining: Remove material to create desired shape Chip formation by cutting tool Advantages: + High accuracy (¬±0.01-0.1 mm) + Excellent surface finish + Versatile (any solid material) + Complex geometries Disadvantages: - Material waste (chips) - Slower than forming - Higher cost for simple shapes Cutting Parameters: Cutting Speed (v): Velocity of tool relative to work surface Turning: v = œÄDn (m/min or ft/min) Where D = diameter, n = spindle speed (rpm) Milling: v = œÄDn Feed Rate (f): Advancement per revolution or per tooth Turning: f = mm/rev Milling: f_z = mm/tooth Depth of Cut (d): Penetration of tool Radial (turning) or axial (milling) Material Removal Rate (MRR): Volume removed per time Turning: MRR = œÄDdf¬∑n = vfd Milling: MRR = a_e¬∑a_p¬∑v_f Where a_e = width, a_p = depth, v_f = feed velocity Cutting Force and Power: Cutting force: F_c = k_s¬∑A_c Where k_s = specific cutting force (material property) A_c = chip cross-section = fd Power: P_c = F_c¬∑v / Œ∑ Where Œ∑ = machine efficiency (‚âà 0.8) Chip Formation: Shear deformation ahead of tool Chip types: Continuous: Ductile, low feed, sharp tool Built-up edge (BUE): Adhesion on tool (bad) Segmented: Medium ductility Discontinuous: Brittle materials Chip thickness ratio: r_c = t_c/t_o (typically 2-5) Where t_c = chip thickness, t_o = uncut thickness Merchant's Analysis: Shear plane angle (œÜ) minimizes energy tan(œÜ) = (r_c¬∑cos(Œ±)) / (1 - r_c¬∑sin(Œ±)) Where Œ± = rake angle

Machining Operations

Turning (Lathe): Rotating workpiece, stationary tool Operations: Facing: Reduce length (radial feed) Turning: Reduce diameter (axial feed) Boring: Enlarge existing hole Grooving: Cut narrow slot Threading: Cut external threads Parting: Cut off part Workholding: Chuck: 3-jaw (self-centering), 4-jaw (independent) Collet: Precision, small parts Faceplate: Large, irregular Centers: Between headstock and tailstock Cutting time: t = L/(fn) (for axial feed) Milling: Rotating tool, moving workpiece Types: Face milling: Cutter axis ‚ä• surface Creates flat surfaces Peripheral (slab) milling: Cutter axis ‚à• surface Side of cutter cuts Up milling (conventional): Chip thickness increases Tool lifts workpiece (needs clamping) Down milling (climb): Chip thickness decreases Better finish, tool pulls down Requires backlash elimination Cutting time: t = L/(f_z¬∑z¬∑n) Where z = number of teeth Drilling: Create round holes Twist drill: Most common Point angle: 118¬∞ (general), 135¬∞ (hard materials) Cutting speed at periphery (v = œÄDn) Feed rate: f mm/rev Depth: For through hole, L = t + D/2 (point) Drilling time: t = L/(fn) Peck drilling: Withdraw periodically (chip removal) Boring: Enlarge and finish existing hole Better accuracy than drilling Single-point boring tool Horizontal or vertical boring machine Reaming: Finish operation for holes Precision (¬±0.01 mm), smooth finish Remove ~0.2-0.5 mm allowance Sawing: Cut stock to length Hacksaw, band saw, circular saw Broaching: Multiple progressive teeth Single pass produces finished surface Internal: Keyways, splines External: Flat surfaces High productivity, expensive tooling Planing and Shaping: Reciprocating tool (shaper) or work (planer) Flat surfaces, large parts Mostly replaced by milling

Tool Materials and Tool Life

Cutting Tool Materials: High-Speed Steel (HSS): Alloy steel, heat treated W, Mo, Cr, V alloying Operating: Up to 600¬∞C (red hardness) Cost: Low Toughness: Excellent Hardness: Good Use: General purpose, drills, taps Cemented Carbide: WC particles in Co binder Hardness: Very high Toughness: Moderate Cost: Medium Cutting speed: 2-3√ó HSS Grades: C-1 to C-4: Roughing to finishing Coatings: TiN, TiC, Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ (improve wear) Use: Inserts, high-production turning/milling Ceramics: Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ, Si‚ÇÉN‚ÇÑ Hardness: Extremely high Hot hardness: Excellent (1000¬∞C+) Toughness: Low (brittle) Very high speeds (5-10√ó HSS) Use: Finishing hard materials Cubic Boron Nitride (CBN): Second hardest to diamond Machine hardened steels (45-65 HRC) High-temperature stability Expensive Diamond: Natural or polycrystalline (PCD) Hardest Best for non-ferrous (Al, Cu, composites) Reacts with Fe at high T Excellent finish Very expensive Tool Life: Time until tool fails (wear, breakage) Wear mechanisms: Flank wear: Rubbing on workpiece Crater wear: Chip flow on rake face Notch wear: At depth of cut line Criteria: Flank wear land width (e.g., 0.3 mm) Taylor Tool Life Equation: vT^n = C Where: v = cutting speed T = tool life (min) n = exponent (material/tool dependent) HSS: n ‚âà 0.1-0.15 Carbide: n ‚âà 0.2-0.4 Ceramic: n ‚âà 0.5-0.7 C = constant Higher speed ‚Üí shorter life (exponential) Extended Form: vT^n¬∑f^a¬∑d^b = C Includes feed (f) and depth (d) Optimum Cutting Speed: Balance productivity and tool cost Maximum production rate: v_opt = C / [T_opt^n] Where T_opt = (1-n)/n ¬∑ (t_c + t_t) t_c = tool change time t_t = tool cost / labor rate Coolants and Lubricants: Functions: - Cool tool and workpiece - Lubricate (reduce friction) - Flush chips - Prevent corrosion Types: Oils: Better lubrication, lower cooling Water-based: Better cooling, rust issues Dry/MQL: Minimal quantity, environmental

Advanced Manufacturing

Grinding and Abrasive Processes

Grinding: Abrasive machining with rotating wheel Advantages: + Very fine finish (Ra < 0.4 Œºm) + High accuracy (¬±0.002 mm) + Machine hardened materials Disadvantages: - Slow material removal - Heat generation - Expensive wheels Grinding Wheel: Abrasive grains bonded together Designation: 51-A-36-L-5-V 51 = manufacturer's prefix A = abrasive type 36 = grit size L = grade (hardness) 5 = structure V = bond type Abrasive Types: Aluminum Oxide (Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ): General steels Silicon Carbide (SiC): Cast iron, non-ferrous CBN: Hardened steels Diamond: Ceramics, carbides, composites Grit Size: Mesh number (8-600) Coarse (8-24): Rough grinding, soft materials Medium (30-60): General purpose Fine (70-180): Finishing Very fine (200-600): Precision, lapping Grade (Hardness): Bond strength (A-Z) Soft (A-H): Hard materials, heavy cuts Medium (I-P): General Hard (Q-Z): Soft materials, light cuts "Soft wheel for hard work, hard wheel for soft work" Grinding Operations: Surface grinding: Flat surfaces Cylindrical grinding: External OD Internal grinding: Holes Centerless grinding: High production, no centers Tool & cutter grinding: Sharpen tools Dressing and Truing: Dressing: Sharpen wheel (remove dulled grains) Truing: Restore wheel shape Diamond dresser common Grinding Ratio: G = Volume of material removed / Volume of wheel wear Typical: G = 20-200 (higher is better)

Non-Traditional Machining

Electrical Discharge Machining (EDM): Spark erosion between tool and workpiece Process: 1. Tool (electrode) and work in dielectric fluid 2. Pulsed DC voltage (50-400 V) 3. Spark discharge melts/vaporizes material 4. Debris flushed away Types: Die-sinking EDM: 3D cavities (molds, dies) Wire EDM: 2D contours (0.02-0.3 mm wire) Advantages: + Machine any conductive material + Complex shapes, fine details + No cutting forces (delicate parts) + Hard materials (carbide, hardened steel) Disadvantages: - Slow (0.1-10 mm¬≥/min) - Conductive materials only - Heat-affected zone - Electrode wear (die-sinking) Applications: Injection mold cavities, turbine blades, microholes Electrochemical Machining (ECM): Reverse electroplating (anodic dissolution) Electrolyte flows between tool (cathode) and work (anode) DC current dissolves workpiece Advantages: + No tool wear + No residual stress + High removal rate Disadvantages: - Corrosive electrolyte - Poor surface finish - Complex process control Laser Beam Machining (LBM): Focused high-energy laser Melts and vaporizes material Lasers: CO‚ÇÇ: Infrared, metals and non-metals Nd:YAG: Near-infrared, metals Fiber: High power, efficiency Applications: Cutting, drilling, welding, marking Sheet metal cutting, microelectronics Water Jet Machining (WJM): High-velocity water stream Pressure: 200-400 MPa Velocity: Up to 900 m/s Nozzle: 0.1-0.4 mm Abrasive WJM (AWJ): Add garnet or aluminum oxide Cuts harder materials Advantages: + No heat (no thermal damage) + Any material (with abrasive) + No tool wear Disadvantages: - Noisy - Slow for thick materials - Taper on edges Applications: Composites, food cutting, stone Ultrasonic Machining (USM): Tool vibrates at ultrasonic frequency (~20 kHz) Abrasive slurry between tool and work Brittle materials: Glass, ceramics Small, intricate shapes

Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing)

Additive Manufacturing (AM): Build parts layer by layer Advantages: + Complex geometries (internal features, organic shapes) + Rapid prototyping + Customization (medical implants) + Minimal waste + No tooling Disadvantages: - Slow for large quantities - Anisotropic properties (layer bonding) - Surface finish (stair-stepping) - Limited materials (vs traditional) - Size limitations Polymer AM: Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM): Extrude thermoplastic filament layer by layer Materials: PLA, ABS, PETG, nylon Low cost, easy Applications: Prototypes, jigs, consumer products Stereolithography (SLA): UV laser cures liquid photopolymer resin High resolution, smooth finish Materials: Resins (standard, tough, flexible, castable) Applications: Jewelry, dental models Selective Laser Sintering (SLS): Laser fuses polymer powder No support structures needed (powder supports) Materials: Nylon, TPU Applications: Functional prototypes, end-use parts Metal AM: Powder Bed Fusion: Laser or electron beam melts metal powder Selective Laser Melting (SLM): Laser fully melts powder Direct Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS): Laser sinters powder Electron Beam Melting (EBM): Electron beam in vacuum Materials: Ti-6Al-4V, AlSi10Mg, stainless steel, Inconel Support structures required (overhang, warping) Applications: Aerospace (topology optimized), medical implants Directed Energy Deposition (DED): Powder or wire fed into focused energy source (laser, arc) Add material to existing part (repair, cladding) Larger parts, multi-axis Binder Jetting: Liquid binder selectively deposited on powder Sinter or infiltrate after printing Fast, large parts, full color possible Lower strength than laser-based Post-Processing: Support removal Heat treatment (stress relief, HIP) Machining (critical surfaces) Surface finishing (polishing, bead blasting) Design for AM (DfAM): Optimize for AM capabilities: - Topology optimization - Lattice structures (lightweight) - Consolidate assemblies - Conformal cooling channels - Overhang limits (typically < 45¬∞ without support)

Summary

  • Casting: sand (low-cost, rough), die (high-production, tight tolerance), investment (complex, precision)
  • Forging: improved grain flow and strength, hot (easier deformation) vs cold (better finish, work hardened)
  • Rolling reduces thickness through rotating rolls, extrusion forces through die for constant cross-section
  • Sheet metal: bending with springback, punching/blanking with clearance, deep drawing limited by LDR‚âà2
  • Machining: turning (rotating work), milling (rotating tool), drilling (create holes), high accuracy and finish
  • Cutting parameters: speed v (surface velocity), feed f (advance per rev), depth d, MRR=vfd for turning
  • Tool life: Taylor vT^n=C, carbide 2-3√ó faster than HSS, ceramics/CBN/diamond for hard materials
  • Non-traditional: EDM (conductive, complex), laser (versatile, no force), waterjet (no heat), ECM (no wear)
  • Welding: arc (SMAW/MIG/TIG), resistance (spot/seam), HAZ affects properties, brazing/soldering lower temperature
  • Additive: FDM (low-cost polymer), SLA (smooth resin), SLS (no supports), metal AM (aerospace/medical)
