Introduction to Materials Science

Materials Science studies the relationships between structure, properties, processing, and performance of engineering materials. Understanding why materials behave as they do enables engineers to select the right material for each application and develop new materials with superior properties.

Atomic Structure and Bonding

Types of Atomic Bonds

Atomic Bonding: Determines material properties Four primary types: 1. Metallic Bonding: - Valence electrons delocalized (electron sea) - Positively charged ion cores - Non-directional Properties: - High electrical/thermal conductivity - Ductile, malleable - Opaque, lustrous Examples: Fe, Cu, Al, Au 2. Ionic Bonding: - Transfer of electrons - Electrostatic attraction between ions - Non-directional Properties: - Hard, brittle - High melting point - Insulator (ceramic becomes conductor when molten) Examples: NaCl, MgO, Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ 3. Covalent Bonding: - Sharing of electrons - Directional (specific angles) - Very strong Properties: - Hard, brittle - High melting point - Poor conductor Examples: Diamond (C), SiC, Si‚ÇÉN‚ÇÑ 4. Van der Waals (Secondary): - Weak intermolecular forces - Dipole interactions Properties: - Low melting point - Soft Examples: Polymers, molecular solids Mixed Bonding: Many materials have combination SiO‚ÇÇ: Covalent + Ionic Graphite: Covalent (in-plane) + Van der Waals (between planes) Bond Strength Impact: Stronger bonds ‚Üí - Higher melting point - Higher elastic modulus - Lower thermal expansion - Higher thermal conductivity (for crystals)

Crystal Structures

Crystalline vs. Amorphous: Crystalline: Atoms arranged in periodic 3D pattern Long-range order Examples: Most metals, salt, diamond Amorphous: Random atomic arrangement No long-range order Examples: Glass, many polymers Unit Cell: Smallest repeating unit Defines crystal structure Parameters: Lattice parameters: a, b, c (edge lengths) Angles: Œ±, Œ≤, Œ≥ Common Metal Structures: 1. Face-Centered Cubic (FCC): - Atoms at corners + face centers - Coordination number: 12 - APF (packing factor): 0.74 (dense) Examples: Al, Cu, Au, Ag, Ni Properties: - Ductile - No ductile-brittle transition 2. Body-Centered Cubic (BCC): - Atoms at corners + body center - Coordination number: 8 - APF: 0.68 Examples: Fe (Œ±), Cr, W, Mo Properties: - Less ductile than FCC - Ductile-brittle transition at low T 3. Hexagonal Close-Packed (HCP): - Atoms in hexagonal layers - Coordination number: 12 - APF: 0.74 (dense like FCC) Examples: Mg, Ti (Œ±), Zn, Co Properties: - Limited slip systems - Less ductile Miller Indices: Notation for crystal planes and directions Plane (hkl): Reciprocals of intercepts Direction [uvw]: Vector components Example: (111) plane, [110] direction Important for: - Slip systems - Cleavage planes - X-ray diffraction Polymorphism (Allotropy): Same element, different structures Fe: BCC (Œ±) < 912¬∞C, FCC (Œ≥) 912-1394¬∞C, BCC (Œ¥) > 1394¬∞C C: Diamond (cubic), Graphite (hexagonal) Basis for steel heat treatment

Summary

  • Bonding determines properties: metallic (ductile, conductive), ionic/covalent (hard, brittle)
  • Crystal structures: FCC and HCP most dense (74%), BCC less (68%), affects ductility via slip systems
  • Defects enable properties: vacancies (diffusion), dislocations (plasticity), grain boundaries (strength)
  • Strengthening: grain refinement (Hall-Petch), solid solution, strain hardening, precipitation, phase transformation
  • Phase diagrams: Fe-C critical for steels, eutectoid (0.76%C, 727¬∞C) forms pearlite
  • Steels: low C (weldable), medium C (heat treatable), high C (tools), stainless (>11% Cr for corrosion)
  • Non-ferrous: Al (lightweight, 2xxx/7xxx aerospace), Ti (high strength/weight, expensive), Cu (conductive)
  • Composites: fiber-reinforced (high specific strength), anisotropic, used where performance justifies cost
  • Selection: mechanical, environmental, manufacturing, economic factors; use Ashby charts and material indices
