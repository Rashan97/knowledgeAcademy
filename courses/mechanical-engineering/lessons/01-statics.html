Introduction to Statics

Statics is the branch of mechanics dealing with bodies at rest or moving at constant velocity. It's the foundation of structural and mechanical analysis, essential for designing everything from bridges to machine frames. Mastering statics is critical for all mechanical engineers.

Moment of a Force

Moment (Torque): Tendency of force to rotate body about a point M = r √ó F (vector cross product) Magnitude (2D): M = F √ó d Where: F = force magnitude d = perpendicular distance from point to line of action Direction: Right-hand rule - Counterclockwise: Positive (+) - Clockwise: Negative (-) Varignon's Theorem: Moment of resultant = Sum of moments of components M_O = r √ó (F‚ÇÅ + F‚ÇÇ) = (r √ó F‚ÇÅ) + (r √ó F‚ÇÇ) Couple: Two parallel forces, equal magnitude, opposite direction Moment of couple: M = F √ó d Where d = perpendicular distance between forces Properties: - Same moment about ANY point - Cannot be balanced by single force - Can be moved anywhere (free vector) Example: Wrench with 50 N force, 0.3 m from bolt M = F √ó d = 50 √ó 0.3 = 15 N¬∑m This torque tightens the bolt Equivalent Force Systems: Two force systems are equivalent if they have: 1. Same resultant force 2. Same resultant moment about any point

Equilibrium of Rigid Bodies

Conditions of Equilibrium

For a body in equilibrium (at rest or constant velocity): 1. Force Equilibrium: Œ£F = 0 (vector sum of all forces = zero) In 2D: Œ£F_x = 0 (sum of horizontal forces) Œ£F_y = 0 (sum of vertical forces) In 3D: Œ£F_x = 0 Œ£F_y = 0 Œ£F_z = 0 2. Moment Equilibrium: Œ£M = 0 (sum of all moments about any point = zero) In 2D: Œ£M_O = 0 (about point O) In 3D: Œ£M_x = 0 Œ£M_y = 0 Œ£M_z = 0 Total: 3 equations in 2D, 6 equations in 3D Number of Unknowns vs. Equations: - Unknowns = Equations: Statically determinate - Unknowns > Equations: Statically indeterminate - Unknowns < Equations: Unstable or inconsistent Two-Force Members: If only two forces act on a body in equilibrium: - Forces must be equal in magnitude - Forces must be opposite in direction - Forces must act along the line joining points Three-Force Members: If three forces act on a rigid body in equilibrium: - Lines of action must be concurrent (meet at a point) - OR all three forces are parallel

Structural Analysis

Trusses - Method of Joints

Truss: Structure composed of two-force members (straight) Connected by pin joints (frictionless) Loads applied only at joints Assumptions: - Members are two-force members - All forces axial (tension or compression) - Weight of members negligible Method of Joints: Analyze equilibrium of each joint sequentially Procedure: 1. Calculate support reactions (FBD of entire truss) 2. Select joint with ‚â§ 2 unknowns 3. Draw FBD of joint (concurrent forces) 4. Apply Œ£F_x = 0 and Œ£F_y = 0 5. Solve for member forces 6. Move to next joint 7. Repeat until all members found Sign Convention: Tension: Positive (+) [pulls on joint, stretches member] Compression: Negative (-) [pushes on joint, shortens member] Zero-Force Members: Conditions for zero force: 1. Joint with 2 non-collinear members, no load ‚Üí both zero 2. Joint with 3 members (2 collinear), no load ‚Üí third is zero Purpose: Provide stability, support during construction Example (simple truss): Warren truss, 3 m span, 1.5 m height Vertical load 1000 N at center joint Members: AB (base), AC and BC (inclined at 45¬∞) Joint C (apex): Œ£F_y = 0: F_AC sin(45¬∞) + F_BC sin(45¬∞) - 1000 = 0 By symmetry: F_AC = F_BC 2F_AC √ó 0.707 = 1000 F_AC = 707 N (tension) F_BC = 707 N (tension) Joint A: Œ£F_x = 0: F_AB + F_AC cos(45¬∞) = 0 F_AB + 707 √ó 0.707 = 0 F_AB = -500 N (compression)

Trusses - Method of Sections

Method of Sections: Cut through truss to find specific member forces Faster when only few members needed Procedure: 1. Calculate support reactions 2. Pass imaginary cutting plane through truss - Must cut through ‚â§ 3 members (for 2D) - Should cut through member of interest 3. Draw FBD of one section 4. Apply equilibrium equations (Œ£F_x, Œ£F_y, Œ£M) 5. Solve for unknown forces Strategy: - Choose section with fewest unknowns - Take moments about intersection of two unknowns (eliminates two unknowns from one equation) Example: Bridge truss, want force in diagonal member DE Cut through DE and two adjacent members Consider left section Take moments about point where other two members intersect Only F_DE appears in equation ‚Üí solve directly Advantages over Method of Joints: - Faster for specific members - Don't need to solve entire truss - Good for internal members Disadvantages: - Need support reactions first - Limited to ‚â§ 3 unknown members per cut

Frames and Machines

Frames: Structures with at least one multi-force member Designed to support loads Stationary Machines: Structures designed to transmit/modify forces Often have moving parts Difference from Trusses: - Members can have forces not aligned with member - Internal forces include shear and bending moment - Joints may have moments Analysis Procedure: 1. Draw FBD of entire structure ‚Üí find reactions 2. Disassemble into individual members 3. Draw FBD of each member - Show all forces (external and internal) - Internal forces appear as pairs (Newton's 3rd law) 4. Apply equilibrium to each member 5. Solve system of equations Pin Connections: - Can transmit force in any direction (F_x, F_y) - Cannot transmit moment (free to rotate) - Two components of force at each pin Multi-Force Member: Three or more forces act on member Forces not necessarily along member axis Must consider moments Example: Simple Pliers Two members hinged at center Applied force at handle ends Gripping force at jaw ends Analyze each handle as separate FBD Pin at hinge provides F_x and F_y Mechanical advantage from geometry

Summary

  • Statics analyzes bodies in equilibrium: Œ£F = 0 and Œ£M = 0

  • Support reactions: roller (1 unknown), pin (2 unknowns), fixed (3 unknowns)
  • Trusses: two-force members, use method of joints or sections
  • Frames and machines: multi-force members, analyze each component separately
  • Friction: F_s ‚â§ Œº_s N (static), F_k = Œº_k N (kinetic), Œº_k < Œº_s always
  • Centroid: geometric center, xÃÑ = Œ£Ax/Œ£A, use composite method for complex shapes

  • Always check units and verify answers with alternative equilibrium equations
