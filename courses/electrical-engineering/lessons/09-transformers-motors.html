Introduction

Transformers and rotating machines (motors and generators) are fundamental to electrical power systems. Transformers enable efficient power transmission by changing voltage levels, while motors convert electrical energy to mechanical work in countless applications.

Transformer Types

Part 3: AC Motors

Three-Phase Induction Motor

Most Common Industrial Motor (70% of all motors) Operating Principle: 1. Stator: Three-phase windings create rotating magnetic field 2. Rotor: Conductors (squirrel cage or wound rotor) 3. Rotating field induces current in rotor 4. Induced current creates magnetic field 5. Rotor field interacts with stator field ‚Üí torque Synchronous Speed: n_s = 120 √ó f / p Where: n_s = synchronous speed (RPM) f = frequency (Hz) p = number of poles Examples (60 Hz): 2-pole: n_s = 3600 RPM 4-pole: n_s = 1800 RPM 6-pole: n_s = 1200 RPM 8-pole: n_s = 900 RPM Slip: s = (n_s - n_r) / n_s Where: n_r = actual rotor speed s = slip (per unit or %) Example: 4-pole motor, n_s = 1800 RPM, actual n_r = 1750 RPM s = (1800 - 1750)/1800 = 0.0278 = 2.78% Typical slip at full load: 2-5% At standstill (starting): s = 1 (100%) At synchronous speed: s = 0 (theoretical, no torque) Rotor Frequency: f_r = s √ó f Example: 2.78% slip, 60 Hz supply f_r = 0.0278 √ó 60 = 1.67 Hz (very slow rotor current) Types: 1. Squirrel Cage Rotor: - Aluminum or copper bars - End rings short bars together - Robust, no maintenance - 90% of induction motors Classes (NEMA): A: Normal starting torque B: Normal (most common) C: High starting torque D: Very high starting torque, high slip 2. Wound Rotor: - Three-phase winding on rotor - Connected to slip rings - External resistance for starting - Higher cost, maintenance - Variable speed applications

Induction Motor Starting

Starting Problem: At start: s = 1, high current (5-7√ó rated) Voltage drop affects other equipment Codes limit starting current Starting Methods: 1. Direct-On-Line (DOL): - Full voltage applied instantly - Simplest, cheapest - Only for small motors (< 5 HP) - High inrush current 2. Star-Delta Starter: - Start in star (Y) connection - Run in delta (Œî) connection - Reduces starting current to 1/3 - Reduces starting torque to 1/3 - Common in Europe 3. Autotransformer Starter: - Reduced voltage tap (50%, 65%, 80%) - Better torque than Y-Œî - More expensive - Medium/large motors 4. Soft Starter (Solid-State): - Thyristor-based voltage control - Gradual voltage ramp - Current limiting - Smooth acceleration - Modern standard for 5-500 HP 5. Variable Frequency Drive (VFD): - Best method (but most expensive) - Controls voltage and frequency - V/f ratio constant ‚Üí constant flux - Wide speed range - Energy savings at reduced speed Speed Control: 1. Pole Changing: - Special winding design - 2 or 4 discrete speeds - Simple, robust - Cooling tower fans, pumps 2. Voltage Control: - Limited range - Poor efficiency - Rarely used 3. Frequency Control (VFD): - Best method - Wide speed range (0-100+ Hz) - Constant torque if V/f maintained - Energy savings (fan/pump loads) P ‚àù n¬≥ for fans/pumps! 50% speed ‚Üí 12.5% power

Example 4: Induction Motor Slip

Problem: 6-pole, 60 Hz induction motor Full-load speed: 1164 RPM Rotor copper loss at full load: 800W Find: Slip, air-gap power, mechanical power developed Solution: Step 1: Synchronous speed n_s = 120 √ó f / p = 120 √ó 60 / 6 = 1200 RPM Step 2: Slip s = (n_s - n_r) / n_s s = (1200 - 1164) / 1200 s = 36 / 1200 = 0.03 = 3% Step 3: Air-gap power P_cu,rotor = s √ó P_gap 800 = 0.03 √ó P_gap P_gap = 800 / 0.03 = 26,667 W ‚âà 26.7 kW Step 4: Mechanical power developed P_mech = P_gap - P_cu,rotor P_mech = 26,667 - 800 = 25,867 W ‚âà 25.9 kW Alternatively: P_mech = (1 - s) √ó P_gap P_mech = (1 - 0.03) √ó 26,667 P_mech = 0.97 √ó 26,667 = 25,867 W ‚úì This shows importance of low slip for efficiency!

Synchronous Motors

Synchronous Motor Characteristics: Principle: - Stator: Three-phase winding (like induction motor) - Rotor: DC-excited electromagnet or permanent magnet - Rotor locks to rotating magnetic field - Runs exactly at synchronous speed (no slip!) Advantages: 1. Constant speed (n = n_s, zero slip) 2. High efficiency (no rotor copper loss from slip) 3. Power factor control: - Over-excited ‚Üí leading PF (supplies VARs) - Under-excited ‚Üí lagging PF - Unity PF possible 4. High power density Disadvantages: 1. Not self-starting (needs special starting method) 2. More complex control 3. Higher cost 4. Requires DC excitation (unless PM) Starting Methods: 1. Damper (amortisseur) windings: - Acts like squirrel cage at start - Motor starts as induction motor - DC applied when near synchronous speed - "Pulls into sync" 2. Variable frequency drive: - Start at low frequency - Gradually increase to 60 Hz - Modern method Applications: - Large compressors (constant speed) - Large pumps - Power factor correction (synchronous condenser) - Precision drives (no slip error) - Industrial loads > 100 HP Types: 1. Wound rotor (slip rings, DC excitation) 2. Permanent magnet (PM synchronous): - No excitation needed - High efficiency - Used in EVs, robotics (servo motors) 3. Reluctance motor: - No excitation, no magnets - Lower performance - Simple, robust

Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs)

VFD Principle: AC ‚Üí DC ‚Üí AC (variable voltage and frequency) Block Diagram: 3-ph AC ‚Üí Rectifier ‚Üí DC Bus ‚Üí Inverter ‚Üí Motor (fixed) (caps) (PWM) (variable) Components: 1. Rectifier: - Diode bridge (6 diodes for 3-phase) - Converts AC to DC - Uncontrolled 2. DC Bus: - Large capacitors - Smooth DC voltage - Energy storage 3. Inverter: - 6 IGBTs (or MOSFETs) - PWM switching (2-20 kHz) - Creates variable AC from DC Control Methods: 1. V/f Control (Scalar): - Maintains V/f ratio constant - Keeps flux constant - Simple, cheap - Open-loop - Accuracy: ¬±3% speed - Most common 2. Vector Control: - Controls magnitude and angle of flux - Fast dynamic response - Better torque control - More complex - Used in servo applications Benefits: 1. Energy savings: - Pumps/fans: Affinity laws - P ‚àù n¬≥ - 20% speed reduction ‚Üí 50% energy savings! 2. Soft starting: - No inrush current - Reduced mechanical stress 3. Process control: - Precise speed/flow regulation - No throttling losses 4. Extended speed range: - 0-120 Hz typical - Constant torque to base speed - Constant power above base Issues: 1. Harmonics: - Non-sinusoidal current - Filters may be needed - IEEE 519 compliance 2. Motor heating: - Reduced cooling at low speed - May need derating 3. Cable length limits: - Reflected waves - dv/dt stress on insulation - Output filters/reactors 4. EMI/RFI: - Shielded cables - Proper grounding Energy Savings Example: 100 HP pump, 10 hrs/day, $0.10/kWh 50% flow with throttling: Still draws ~85 HP ‚Üí 634 kW-hr/day ‚Üí $63.40/day 50% flow with VFD: Draws ~12.5 HP (50%¬≥) ‚Üí 93 kW-hr/day ‚Üí $9.30/day Savings: $54.10/day √ó 365 = $19,750/year! VFD payback: Often < 2 years

üí° Motor Selection Guide

  • DC Motors: Legacy systems, precise speed control, high starting torque
  • Induction (squirrel cage): General purpose, 90% of applications, robust
  • Induction + VFD: Variable speed, energy savings, process control
  • Synchronous: Constant speed, PF correction, large loads (>100 HP)
  • PM Synchronous: High efficiency, servo applications, EVs, robotics
  • Single-phase induction: Residential (< 2 HP), split-phase or capacitor-start

Summary

  • Transformers change voltage levels using electromagnetic induction (Faraday's Law)
  • Ideal transformer: V‚ÇÅ/V‚ÇÇ = N‚ÇÅ/N‚ÇÇ = a, I‚ÇÅ/I‚ÇÇ = 1/a, Z_in = a¬≤Z_load
  • Real transformers have copper losses (I¬≤R) and core losses (hysteresis + eddy currents)
  • Three-phase transformers: Œî-Y most common for distribution, provides neutral and grounding
  • DC motors: Series (high starting torque), Shunt (constant speed), Compound (versatile)
  • Speed control: Armature voltage (below base), field flux (above base), PWM modern method
  • Induction motors: Most common (70%), robust, self-starting, 2-5% slip at full load
  • Synchronous speed: n_s = 120f/p; Slip: s = (n_s - n_r)/n_s
  • VFD provides energy savings (P ‚àù n¬≥ for pumps/fans), soft starting, wide speed range
  • Synchronous motors run at exact n_s, can correct power factor, used for large constant-speed loads
