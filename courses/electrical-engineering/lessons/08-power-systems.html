Three-Phase Power Systems

Why Three-Phase?

Advantages of Three-Phase over Single-Phase: 1. More efficient power transfer - 3-phase uses 75% less conductor material for same power - Constant instantaneous power (smooth torque in motors) 2. Smaller equipment size - Motors and generators are smaller for same power rating - More power per unit weight 3. Easier to start motors - Rotating magnetic field automatically produced - No starting mechanism needed 4. Balanced system - Currents sum to zero in neutral - Can use smaller neutral conductor 5. Economic advantages - Lower installation costs - Reduced transmission losses - Higher transmission capacity Applications: - Power generation (all large plants) - Industrial facilities - Commercial buildings - Three-phase motors (pumps, compressors, HVAC) Single-phase used for: - Residential homes (< 200A service) - Small loads - Lighting circuits

Protection Systems

Types of Faults

Common Power System Faults: 1. Three-Phase Fault (balanced): - All three phases shorted together - Least common (< 5% of faults) - Most severe, highest current - Symmetrical - easy to analyze 2. Line-to-Line Fault: - Two phases shorted together - ~15% of faults - Asymmetrical 3. Single Line-to-Ground Fault: - One phase to ground/earth - Most common (70-80% of faults) - Severity depends on grounding system - May be self-clearing (tree contact) 4. Double Line-to-Ground Fault: - Two phases shorted to ground - ~10% of faults Fault Causes: - Lightning strikes - Insulation breakdown - Animal contact - Tree branches - Equipment failure - Human error - Dig-ins (underground cables) Fault Currents: Can be 10-100Ã— normal current Typical: 5,000A to 50,000A at distribution level Must be cleared quickly (< 0.1 second)

Protective Devices

Protection Hierarchy: 1. Fuses: - Simple, cheap, reliable - One-time use (must replace) - Time-current characteristic - Types: Expulsion, current-limiting - Used: Distribution, motor protection 2. Circuit Breakers: - Automatic operation - Reclosable - Trip mechanisms: * Thermal: Overload (slow) * Magnetic: Short circuit (fast) * Electronic: Programmable - Ratings: Voltage, current, interrupting capacity 3. Relays: - Protective logic/intelligence - Monitor voltage, current, frequency - Send trip signal to breaker Types: a) Overcurrent Relay (50/51): - Instantaneous (50): I > threshold â†’ trip - Time-delayed (51): IDMT curve b) Distance Relay (21): - Measures impedance Z = V/I - Protects transmission lines - Zone protection c) Differential Relay (87): - Compares current in/out - Protects transformers, generators - Very sensitive, fast d) Under/Over Voltage (27/59) e) Under/Over Frequency (81) f) Ground Fault (50G/51G) 4. Reclosers: - Automatic circuit breakers - Multiple reclose attempts - Clears temporary faults - Used on distribution feeders Coordination: Devices must operate in sequence Closest device to fault opens first Backup protection if primary fails Time-current curves ensure selectivity

Grounding Systems

Purpose of Grounding: 1. Safety: Limit touch/step voltages 2. Protection: Fault current path 3. System stability: Reference potential 4. Lightning protection Types of Grounding: 1. Equipment Grounding: - Green wire / bare conductor - Connects equipment frames to earth - Prevents shock if insulation fails - Required by NEC 2. System Grounding: - Connects neutral to earth - Establishes reference - Types: a) Solidly Grounded: - Neutral directly to earth - Low impedance path - High fault current - US residential/commercial b) Resistance Grounded: - Resistor in neutral - Limits fault current - Detects ground faults - Industrial systems c) Ungrounded (isolated): - No intentional ground - Can operate with one fault - Second fault is hazardous - Rare in US 3. Lightning Protection: - Rods, masts, shield wires - Low-impedance path to earth - Surge arresters Grounding Electrode System: - Ground rods (5/8" Ã— 8 ft typical) - Ground rings (buried conductor) - Concrete-encased electrode (Ufer) - Metal water pipe - Ground resistance: < 25Î© (NEC), < 5Î© (telecom)
