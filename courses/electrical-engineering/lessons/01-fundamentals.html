Introduction to Electrical Engineering

Electrical engineering is the study and application of electricity, electronics, and electromagnetism. It's one of the newest engineering disciplines, emerging in the late 19th century with the commercialization of electric power and telegraphy.

Current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

üí° Current Flow Direction

Though we now know electrons (negative) are the actual charge carriers in conductors, we still use conventional current (positive to negative) established by Benjamin Franklin before electrons were discovered. This convention works perfectly for circuit analysis!

3. Voltage (V)

Ohm's Law applies to linear, ohmic materials where resistance is constant. It does NOT apply to:

  • Diodes and transistors (non-linear devices)
  • Thermistors (resistance changes with temperature)
  • Gas discharge lamps
  • Semiconductors in general

Kirchhoff's Laws

Two fundamental laws that govern all electrical circuits, named after Gustav Kirchhoff (1845).

Most circuit analysis uses both laws simultaneously:

Electrical Safety Fundamentals

Understanding electrical hazards is critical for every electrical engineer.

Current Effects on Human Body (60 Hz AC): < 1 mA: Barely perceptible 1-5 mA: Threshold of perception, tingling 5-10 mA: Painful shock 10-20 mA: Muscle contractions, "can't let go" 20-50 mA: Severe pain, breathing difficulty 50-100 mA: Possible ventricular fibrillation (fatal) > 100 mA: Certain ventricular fibrillation, death Resistance of human body: 1,000 - 100,000 Œ© (varies with moisture, contact area, path) Example danger calculation: V = 120V, R_body = 1000Œ© (wet conditions) I = V/R = 120V / 1000Œ© = 120 mA (LETHAL!)

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Rules

  1. De-energize before working - Turn off and lockout power
  2. Verify de-energization - Test with voltmeter
  3. Discharge capacitors - They store charge even when off
  4. One hand rule - Keep one hand in pocket to prevent cross-body path
  5. Insulated tools - Use proper equipment rated for voltage
  6. Dry conditions - Water dramatically lowers body resistance
  7. GFCIs - Use Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters in wet areas
  8. Never assume - Always test before touching

Summary

  • Charge (Q) measured in Coulombs, fundamental property of matter
  • Current (I) is rate of charge flow, measured in Amperes
  • Voltage (V) is potential difference, measured in Volts
  • Resistance (R) opposes current flow, measured in Ohms
  • Power (P) is rate of energy transfer, measured in Watts
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR (fundamental relationship)
  • KCL: Current in equals current out at any node
  • KVL: Voltage rises equal voltage drops in any loop
  • Electrical safety is paramount - understand hazards and precautions
