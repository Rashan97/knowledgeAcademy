Introduction

Sentence structure refers to how words and phrases are arranged to create meaningful sentences. Understanding different sentence types and patterns will help you vary your writing and express ideas more effectively.

Basic Sentence Elements

1. Independent Clause

A group of words that contains a subject and verb and expresses a complete thought. It can stand alone as a sentence.

Examples:

  • The cat sleeps.
  • Students study hard.
  • She enjoys reading.

2. Dependent Clause

A group of words that contains a subject and verb but does NOT express a complete thought. It cannot stand alone.

Examples:

  • Because she was tired
  • When the bell rang
  • Although it was raining

Four Sentence Structures

1. Simple Sentences

Structure: One independent clause

Formula: Subject + Verb (+ Object/Complement)

Examples:

  • The dog barks.
  • Sarah writes novels.
  • The children played in the park.

Note: A simple sentence can have compound subjects or verbs:

  • John and Mary walked to school. (compound subject)
  • She sang and danced. (compound verb)

2. Compound Sentences

Structure: Two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction or semicolon

Formula: Independent Clause + Coordinator + Independent Clause

Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS): for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so

Examples:

  • I wanted to go, but it was raining.
  • She studied hard, so she passed the exam.
  • The sun was shining; birds were singing.

3. Complex Sentences

Structure: One independent clause + one or more dependent clauses

Formula: Independent Clause + Dependent Clause (or vice versa)

Common Subordinators: because, although, when, if, since, while, after, before, unless

Examples:

  • Because it was late, we went home.
  • She smiled when she saw her friend.
  • Although he was tired, he finished his work.

4. Compound-Complex Sentences

Structure: Two or more independent clauses + one or more dependent clauses

Formula: Multiple independent clauses + at least one dependent clause

Examples:

  • Although it was raining, we went to the park, and we had fun.
  • She finished her homework, but she didn't submit it because she forgot.

Sentence Patterns

English sentences follow common patterns based on the relationship between subject, verb, and complements:

Pattern Structure Example
S-V Subject + Verb Birds fly.
S-V-O Subject + Verb + Object She reads books.
S-V-C Subject + Verb + Complement He is happy.
S-V-O-O Subject + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object She gave me a book.
S-V-O-C Subject + Verb + Object + Complement They elected her president.

Phrases vs. Clauses

Phrase

A group of words that does NOT contain both a subject and a verb.

Examples:

  • in the morning (prepositional phrase)
  • running quickly (verb phrase)
  • the big house (noun phrase)

Clause

A group of words that DOES contain both a subject and a verb.

Examples:

  • when she arrived (dependent clause)
  • the dog barked (independent clause)
  • because it was raining (dependent clause)

Common Sentence Errors

1. Sentence Fragment

An incomplete sentence that lacks a subject, verb, or complete thought.

2. Run-on Sentence

Two or more independent clauses joined without proper punctuation or conjunction.

‚úÖ Correct: She studied hard; she passed the exam.

3. Comma Splice

Two independent clauses joined with only a comma.

‚ùå Incorrect: It was raining, we stayed inside.

‚úÖ Correct: It was raining, so we stayed inside.

Tips for Effective Sentence Structure

  1. Vary sentence length: Mix short and long sentences for better flow
  2. Vary sentence structure: Use all four types to maintain interest
  3. Start with different elements: Don't always begin with the subject
  4. Use transitions: Connect sentences logically
  5. Avoid fragments: Ensure each sentence is complete
  6. Check for run-ons: Properly connect independent clauses

Key Takeaways

  • There are four main sentence structures: simple, compound, complex, compound-complex
  • Independent clauses can stand alone; dependent clauses cannot
  • Coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS) join independent clauses
  • Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses
  • Avoid sentence fragments, run-ons, and comma splices
  • Varying sentence structure improves writing quality
